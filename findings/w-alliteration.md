# W-Alliteration in Beowulf: Callie Speaking

I set up Claude Code (Opus 4.6) to use the MCP server in this project (version 0.1.1) for its text and translation.

I was inspired to write this prompt by line 17, "wuldres wealdend worldare forgeaf," one of my absolute favorites in the poem for its leaping, cascading prosody.

My prompt was:
```claude-code
"wuldres wealden, worldaere forgeaf" is a great rhyme.
look for similar rhymes with w sounds that extend through both half-lines.
```

## Claude's Response

Lines where the 'w' sound extends through both half-lines of the Old English verse, inspired by line 17's *wuldres wealdend woroldare forgeaf*.

## Why 'W' Specifically

The poet has a love affair with this consonant. The lip-rounding of 'w' physically mimics waves and wind, and semantically, the poem's biggest themes all start with it:

- **wyrd** -- fate
- **wig** -- war
- **wuldor** -- glory
- **wea** -- woe
- **wine** -- friend/lord
- **wordhord** -- speech
- **wael** -- slaughter
- **waeter** -- water
- **weald** -- power
- **wynne** -- joy

The 'w' does double duty as sound and meaning throughout the poem.

The **worda/worca** pairing (lines 289, 1100, 1833) is especially striking -- "words and works" as a formulaic doublet where the near-rhyme is structural, not accidental. The poet uses it three times across 1,500 lines, each time in a different moral context.

The densest concentrations of 'w' alliteration appear in passages about the sea and weather (where the lip-rounding is onomatopoetic), Hrothgar's sermon on pride and fate (where **wyrd**, **wuldor**, and **wea** collide), and the final battle and funeral (where **wyrm**, **wael**, **wop**, and **wind** converge on Beowulf's death).

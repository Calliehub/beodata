# Finding Kennings - Callie Speaking

This is my first use case with Claude Code (Opus 4.6) configured to use this project's MCP server for its text and translations.

My prompt was:

```claude-code
    look in the whole text for kennings in old english,
    show lines where they occur,
    summarize the meaning of each
```

## Claude's Response

A catalog of Old English kennings (compound metaphorical expressions) found throughout the 3,182 lines of Beowulf, organized by referent.

## Distribution & Observations

The poem contains **well over 200 distinct kennings**, unevenly distributed across the text.

### Densest Concentrations

- **Lines 200-300** (the sea voyage) — ship and sea kennings pile up relentlessly
- **Lines 700-820** (Grendel's attack on Heorot) — body, hall, and combat kennings
- **Lines 1500-1620** (underwater fight with Grendel's mother) — armor and sword kennings everywhere, because the poet needs 15 different ways to say "mail-coat"
- **Lines 2500-2600** (the dragon fight) — the beast gets a new name practically every line
- **Lines 2700-2800** (Beowulf's death) — body and mortality kennings dominate

### Most Productive Categories

- **Kings and lords** (30+) — the poet never just says "king" when he can say "gold-friend of men," "ring-breaker," or "folk's shepherd"
- **The sea** (18+) — one word for "ocean" is for amateurs
- **Swords and weapons** (15+) — a sword is never just a sword but a "battle-gleam," a "war-friend," a "wave-patterned" thing
- **The dragon** (15+) — earns more distinct epithets in 700 lines than most characters get in an entire poem

### Patterns

Kennings tend to define things by their **function** (a king gives rings, guards hoards, and shepherds folk), their **material** (a ship is sea-wood, a shield is linden-wood), or their **metaphorical resemblance** (the sun is a candle, the body is a bone-house, blood is battle-sweat). The same noun rarely appears twice with the same kenning — the poet treats repetition as failure, cycling through compound after compound as if the language itself demands variety.
